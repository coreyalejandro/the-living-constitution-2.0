# Insight Quality Rubric (IQR) — Scoring Instrument and Calibration Package

**Module:** NARRATIVE-CONDITIONED-INTERFACES  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-06-19  
**truth_status:** PROPOSED — ICC not yet measured; anchor scores are design targets

---

## Purpose

The IQR measures the quality of a final analytical insight — the output of Stage 5
of the Narrative-First Adversarial Loop (NFL). Three blind expert raters independently
score each insight on 6 dimensions. Target ICC ≥ 0.86 (two-way mixed, absolute
agreement, average measures).

IQR is the primary outcome measure in the proposed pilot study (N=24) and the
full 3-condition evaluation (N=240).

---

## Construct Definition: What Is a High-Quality Insight?

An insight is not a finding. It is not a summary of what the data shows. A
high-quality insight:

- Was not obvious before the analysis
- Cannot be reduced to a simpler description without losing meaning
- Is actionable — a decision-maker can act differently because of it
- Is grounded — specific evidence is cited, not general patterns
- Is culturally situated — it respects the social, historical, or
  organizational context of the people it describes
- Is narratively coherent — it holds together as a story with a mechanism,
  not just a correlation

The six IQR dimensions operationalize these properties.

| 2 | Slightly unexpected — contradicts a common assumption but is easily explained in retrospect |
| 3 | Non-obvious — requires the specific data to reach; a practitioner could not have stated it before seeing it |
| 4 | Genuinely surprising — contradicts a well-established assumption; would cause a practitioner to update their model of the domain |

### Dimension 2: Depth

*Does the insight explain a mechanism, not just describe a pattern?*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Descriptive only: "X is higher in group A than group B" |
| 2 | Pattern with partial explanation: "X is higher in group A, probably because of Y" |
| 3 | Mechanism stated: "X is higher in group A because Y creates condition Z, which produces the observed pattern; this is consistent with [evidence]" |
| 4 | Mechanism + boundary conditions: above, plus specification of when/where the mechanism does and does not apply |

Score: ___

### Dimension 3: Cultural Situatedness

*Does the insight respect the social, historical, demographic, or organizational
context of the people it describes? Does it avoid extractive or decontextualized
interpretation?*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | No contextual grounding; treats people as undifferentiated data points |
| 2 | Acknowledges context in passing but does not integrate it into the interpretation |
| 3 | Context is integrated: the mechanism explanation includes social/organizational/historical factors relevant to this specific population |
| 4 | Context is central: the insight could not be stated without the contextual grounding; interpretation is specific to this community or organization and explicitly non-transferable without re-validation |

Score: ___

---

### Dimension 4: Evidence Integration

*Is the insight grounded in specific, cited evidence? Does it distinguish what
the evidence shows from what is inferred?*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | No specific evidence cited; general assertion only |
| 2 | Evidence cited but not distinguished from interpretation; conflation of observation and claim |
| 3 | Specific evidence cited; observation clearly separated from interpretation; confidence is appropriate to the evidence |
| 4 | Evidence cited with explicit limitations; not-claimed items are stated; rival hypotheses addressed; confidence is formally bounded |

Score: ___

---

### Dimension 5: Actionability

*Can a decision-maker act differently because of this insight? Is the action
path clear?*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | No clear action implied; informational only |
| 2 | Action implied but vague: "invest more in X" or "pay attention to Y" |
| 3 | Specific action described: who acts, on what, at what threshold, and what the expected effect is |
| 4 | Action + decision criteria: above, plus explicit specification of what would cause the action to be revised or reversed |

Score: ___

---

### Dimension 6: Narrative Coherence

*Does the insight hold together as a coherent story with a beginning (situation),
middle (complication or tension), and resolution (interpretation)?*

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Fragmented: list of observations with no connecting thread |
| 2 | Partial narrative: observations connected but tension or resolution is missing |
| 3 | Complete narrative: situation → complication → interpretation is clear and follows logically |
| 4 | Compelling narrative: above, plus the structure makes the insight memorable and transferable — a colleague could retell it accurately from memory |

## Rater Calibration Procedure

### Timeline: 2.5-hour session

**Part 1 — Conceptual Orientation (45 min)**

Walk through each dimension with the definitions above. Emphasize two common
errors:

- **Conflating Depth and Surprise.** A highly surprising insight may be shallow
  (an outlier with no explanation). A deep insight may not be surprising (a
  mechanism that confirms prior theory but is now evidenced). Score independently.
- **Conflating Evidence Integration and Actionability.** Thorough evidence can
  support a conclusion with no clear action. An actionable insight may rest on
  thin evidence. Score independently.

**Part 2 — Practice Round (60 min)**

Raters independently score the 5 exemplars below. After each, facilitator
reveals anchor scores and walks through the rationale for each dimension.

Target: ≤ 1 point divergence on each dimension for ≥ 80% of dimension-insight
combinations.

**Part 3 — Calibration Check (45 min)**

Raters independently score 3 new test insights not used in training.
Calculate two-way mixed ICC (absolute agreement, average measures).

- Accept: ICC ≥ 0.86
- Conditional: ICC 0.75–0.85 — resolve disputed dimensions via consensus scoring
  protocol (raters discuss, reach documented consensus, document the rule)
- Fail: ICC < 0.75 — identify which dimensions drive disagreement; re-train on
  those dimensions only; re-test before study launch

## Annotated Exemplars — Calibration Cases

*Domain: Health analytics / public health research. All examples are constructed.*

---

### Exemplar 1 — IQR Total: 8 (WEAK)

**Insight text:**
"Patients in the 65+ age group had lower medication adherence rates (72%) compared
to the 45–64 group (81%) in Q3."

**D1 Surprise: 1** — Any health practitioner expects older patients to have
adherence challenges. Confirms prior knowledge.

**D2 Depth: 1** — Pure description. No mechanism, no explanation of why the
gap exists.

**D3 Cultural Situatedness: 1** — No context about this specific population.
"65+" is treated as a homogeneous data category.

**D4 Evidence Integration: 2** — Numbers cited (72%, 81%), but no confidence
interval, no population size, no specification of what "adherence" measures.
Observation and claim are not distinguished.

**D5 Actionability: 2** — Implies investment in elder-care adherence programs
but does not specify what, for whom, at what threshold.

**D6 Narrative Coherence: 1** — Single statistic. No narrative arc.

**Total: 8 — WEAK.** This is a finding, not an insight.

---

### Exemplar 2 — IQR Total: 13 (DEVELOPING)

**Insight text:**
"Patients in zip codes with the lowest pharmacy density had adherence rates
18 percentage points below the study median, suggesting access barriers are
a primary driver of the age-related adherence gap. This is consistent with
the distribution of pharmacy closures in low-income urban corridors over
the past five years."

**D1 Surprise: 2** — Access barriers are a known factor, but the magnitude
(18pp gap tied to pharmacy density rather than patient age per se) reframes
the problem somewhat. Slightly unexpected.

**D2 Depth: 3** — Mechanism stated: pharmacy closures → access barriers →
adherence gap. Consistent with external trend data.

**D3 Cultural Situatedness: 2** — Low-income urban corridors acknowledged,
but no specificity about who lives there or what access means in their
context (transport, work schedule, caregiver burden).

**D4 Evidence Integration: 3** — 18pp gap cited with a comparison base;
mechanism grounded in external trend data. Does not specify confidence
interval or sample size.

**D5 Actionability: 2** — Implies pharmacy access intervention, but no
specificity: which corridors, what intervention type, at what threshold of
density, with what expected adherence effect.

**D6 Narrative Coherence: 1** — Two observations connected but no resolution.
No clear ending: what does this mean and what should happen?

**Total: 13 — DEVELOPING.** Mechanism is present but evidence is thin,
cultural context is surface-level, and the narrative is incomplete.

---

### Exemplar 3 — IQR Total: 17 (PROFICIENT)

**Insight text:**
"Among patients in the Riverside corridor (n=847, 3 clinics), adherence
gaps are not primarily explained by age or income. They are explained by a
trust deficit: patients whose care team changed in the past 12 months
(38% of the cohort) had adherence rates 23pp below patients with continuity
of care (p < .001, controlling for age, income, insurance status, and
pharmacy access). This is not a care management problem — it is a
relationship disruption problem, concentrated in clinics that experienced
staffing turnover above 40% in 2024–2025."

**D1 Surprise: 3** — Reattributes the gap from age/income/access (the
expected explanations) to care-team disruption. Requires the specific
data to reach; non-obvious.

**D2 Depth: 4** — Mechanism (relationship disruption → trust deficit →
non-adherence) stated explicitly. Boundary condition identified: this applies
to patients with care-team changes, not the general population.

**D3 Cultural Situatedness: 3** — Specific corridor (Riverside), specific
clinics, specific staffing context. Not generalizable without re-validation.

**D4 Evidence Integration: 3** — Sample size given (n=847), statistical
significance reported (p < .001), controls specified. Missing: effect
size, confidence interval, how "care team change" is defined and measured.

**D5 Actionability: 3** — Action is specific: address staffing continuity
in clinics with >40% turnover. Decision-maker can act on this. Missing:
what threshold triggers action, what the expected adherence recovery is.

**D6 Narrative Coherence: 1** — Strong mechanism but no narrative arc.
Reads as a data summary, not a story. No situation/complication/resolution
structure.

**Total: 17 — PROFICIENT.** Strong analytical work. Narrative structure
would push this to Strong or Exceptional.

### Exemplar 4 — IQR Total: 21 (STRONG)

**Insight text:**
"In 2019, Riverside's three primary care clinics served a community that
had watched two anchor institutions — a hospital and a community health
center — close in four years. By 2024, the clinic network had absorbed
their displaced patient panels while undergoing its own staffing crisis.
What looks like an adherence problem is actually accumulated institutional
grief: patients who have had care relationships interrupted repeatedly have
developed a rational strategy of low-investment engagement — following
instructions minimally, missing refills at the margin — as protection
against the next disruption. Our data shows this directly: every additional
care-team transition in the past 24 months reduces adherence probability
by 8pp (95% CI: 5–11pp; n=847, 3 clinics), independent of all standard
controls. The action is not a reminder intervention. The action is a
continuity guarantee: assign a named care liaison who persists across
staffing changes and is explicitly introduced to the patient as persistent.
We recommend piloting this in the two highest-turnover clinics first,
with a 6-month adherence comparison against matched controls."

**D1 Surprise: 4** — Reframes adherence as rational adaptation to
institutional disruption. Contradicts the assumption that adherence is a
patient behavior problem.

**D2 Depth: 4** — Mechanism (institutional grief → rational low-investment
engagement → non-adherence) + boundary condition (applies to patients with
repeated care-team transitions, not single disruptions) + quantified effect.

**D3 Cultural Situatedness: 4** — Historical context (hospital/CHC
closures), community-specific framing (accumulated institutional grief),
explicit non-generalizability. Cannot be stated without this context.

**D4 Evidence Integration: 4** — Effect size (8pp per transition), 95% CI
(5–11pp), sample size (n=847), controls specified. Observation (data
pattern) explicitly distinguished from interpretation (rational adaptation
as mechanism).

**D5 Actionability: 4** — Specific action (named care liaison), explicit
deployment criteria (two highest-turnover clinics), evaluation design
(6-month comparison with matched controls). Includes what would cause
revision: if pilot shows no effect, the rational-adaptation mechanism
would need to be reconsidered.

**D6 Narrative Coherence: 1** — Narrative structure is strong — history,
disruption, rational response, evidence, action — but the final resolution
is a recommendation, not a story conclusion. Rater discretion: some may
score this 2 due to the historical opening providing a situation frame.
Anchor: 1 because it reads as a report section, not a transferable story.

**Total: 21 — STRONG.** Near-exceptional on mechanism, context, evidence,
and action. Narrative structure drops it from Exceptional.

### Exemplar 5 — IQR Total: 23 (EXCEPTIONAL)

**Insight text:**
"Riverside is not struggling with a medication adherence problem.
It is struggling with a community that has learned, correctly, that
health institutions leave.

Since 2019, patients at our three clinics have watched two anchor
institutions close, absorbed their displaced neighbors into our already-
strained panels, and cycled through 43% annual staff turnover. What they
have built in response is not passivity — it is a protection strategy:
minimum viable engagement, rationed trust, calculated non-investment in
relationships that will probably not last.

We see this clearly in the data. Each additional care-team transition in
the past 24 months reduces a patient's probability of filling a prescription
at the margin by 8 percentage points (95% CI: 5–11; n=847; p < .001;
controls: age, income, insurance, pharmacy proximity, chronic condition
count). The effect is monotonic: one transition, 8pp. Two transitions,
16pp. Three or more: 29pp. There is no recovery effect in our data window.

This is not a behavior change problem. You cannot nudge your way out of
rational adaptation to institutional abandonment.

The action is a structural guarantee: assign a named care liaison — one
person, visible, persistent across staffing transitions, explicitly
introduced to the patient as 'the person who stays' — to every patient
who has experienced two or more care-team changes in 24 months. Pilot
this in Clinics A and B (highest turnover; N=312 eligible patients).
Measure refill rate at 90 days against matched controls from Clinic C.
If the effect is real, you will see it there first.

If the pilot shows no effect, we were wrong about the mechanism.
The data pattern is solid; the explanation may not be. That is what
the pilot is for."

**D1 Surprise: 4** — Reframes adherence as institutional grief and rational
adaptation. Contradicts the behavioral-science framing dominant in clinical
literature. The monotonic dose-response (8, 16, 29pp) is unexpected.

**D2 Depth: 4** — Mechanism (rational adaptation to abandonment), boundary
condition (2+ transitions in 24 months, no recovery), quantified dose-
response, explicit statement of where the mechanism might be wrong.

**D3 Cultural Situatedness: 4** — History of closures, community absorption
of displaced patients, explicit framing of the community as rational actors
not passive patients. Inseparable from this specific community and this
specific history.

**D4 Evidence Integration: 4** — Monotonic dose-response with point estimates
(8, 16, 29pp), CI, sample size, full control list. Observation explicitly
distinguished from interpretation. Not-claimed item stated: "we were wrong
about the mechanism" is a genuine falsifiability statement.

**D5 Actionability: 4** — Specific action (named care liaison), population
(2+ transitions in 24 months, n=312), pilot design (Clinics A+B vs. Clinic
C matched controls), outcome measure (refill rate at 90 days), explicit
revision criterion (if pilot null, mechanism is wrong).

**D6 Narrative Coherence: 4** — Situation (institutions leave), complication
(patients learned to not invest), resolution (guarantee persistence). Leads
with the story, not the data. A colleague could retell this accurately —
"Riverside patients aren't non-adherent, they're protecting themselves" —
and would not lose the core.

**Total: 23 — EXCEPTIONAL.** All six dimensions near-maximum. Publishable
as a case study in applied analytical reasoning.
